When Gucci Found Your Grandmother's Carpet

The $4,000 Gucci tote that went viral last season bore a pattern that carpet weavers in Tabriz have been knotting for three centuries. Their names are not in the press release. Their city is not in the footnotes. Their craft is, however, very much in the profit margin.

The Vaults That Refused to Disappear

Muqarnas, the geometric honeycomb vaulting developed in Persian and Islamic architecture, has appeared in the work of contemporary architects and luxury interior designers for decades. The original craftspeople who refined the form over centuries are rarely part of that conversation.

Paisley Was Never British

The boteh motif, a curved teardrop shape with roots in Iranian art dating back to the Sassanid period, was mass-produced by Scottish mills in the 19th century and renamed paisley. The name stuck. The origin didn't.

The Badgir: A 3,000-Year-Old Air Conditioner the World Finally Wants

The badgir, a wind-catching tower designed to passively cool buildings, has been in use across Iran for thousands of years. As climate concerns push architects toward passive cooling solutions, the badgir is being studied and adapted worldwide.

The Brand That Built Its Identity on a 14th-Century Manuscript

The Shahnameh, the Persian epic poem completed by Firdausi around 1010 CE, remains one of the most illustrated manuscripts in history. Its visual language, gold-leaf illumination, intricate borders, and jewel-toned miniatures, has quietly influenced branding and editorial design far beyond Iran.

Designing in Two Languages: Behnaz Sarafpour on the Hyphen

Behnaz Sarafpour is an Iranian-American fashion designer who has worked at the highest levels of the industry. Her career sits at the intersection of two visual cultures, and the tension between them has shaped everything she makes.

Zaha Hadid's Persian Inheritance

Her biomorphic curves didn't come from nowhere. They came from Yazd — from the muqarnas ceilings and the sinuous geometry of Safavid tilework that she absorbed before the world ever heard her name.

The Color That Built an Empire

Safavid blue — the deep lapis lazuli hue that covers the domes of Isfahan — was once the most expensive pigment in the world. It still appears on luxury packaging today, usually without credit.

Persian Gardens and the Myth of the English Lawn

The formal garden — symmetrical, walled, centered on water — was a Persian invention. The chahar bagh, or fourfold garden, spread westward through Moorish Spain and Mughal India long before Versailles was a glint in Louis XIV's eye.

What Saffron Actually Costs

Iran produces over 90% of the world's saffron. The spice sells for thousands of dollars per kilogram on Western markets. The farmers who grow it in Khorasan earn a fraction of that. A reckoning on the spice trade's hidden margins.

The Arabesque Is Not Arabic

The repeating geometric floral pattern the West calls arabesque was developed across the medieval Islamic world — with Iranian craftspeople at the center of its most sophisticated iterations. The name stuck. The credit didn't.

On Wearing Your Heritage in a Hostile Room

For Iranian-American artists and designers, referencing Persian visual culture in Western professional spaces is never a neutral act. It invites exoticization, misreading, and sometimes erasure. A look at what it costs to show up fully.
